# Acute Chest Pain Evaluation Guideline

## Symptoms and Presentation

Acute coronary syndrome often presents with chest pain or chest pressure. Chest pain radiating to the left arm, diaphoresis, nausea, and dyspnea are classic findings that support acute coronary syndrome and myocardial infarction.

Pulmonary embolism can present with dyspnea, pleuritic pain, tachycardia, and chest pain. Pleuritic pain and sudden dyspnea support considering pulmonary embolism.

Aortic dissection is an emergent cause of chest pain. Tearing chest pain or ripping chest pain radiating to the back is concerning for aortic dissection.

Gastroesophageal reflux disease can cause burning chest discomfort and heartburn. Diaphoresis and left arm radiation are less typical and may argue against gastroesophageal reflux disease when cardiac symptoms are present.

## Diagnostic Tests

Initial evaluation for acute coronary syndrome should obtain a 12-lead ECG and serial cardiac troponin. A normal initial test does not exclude acute coronary syndrome when symptoms remain concerning.

When pulmonary embolism is clinically suspected, risk stratification and CT pulmonary angiography may be used to evaluate pulmonary embolism.

When aortic dissection is suspected, urgent CT angiography or equivalent aortic imaging should be performed.

## Differential and Red Flags

For acute chest pain, dangerous alternatives include acute coronary syndrome, pulmonary embolism, and aortic dissection. These emergent diagnoses should be considered before low-risk explanations.
